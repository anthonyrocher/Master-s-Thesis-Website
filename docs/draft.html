Let denote \(p\) the subjective probability that a property will be hit by an earthquake. \(p\) depends on both the information set \(i\) individuals possess about the seismic risks (such as a recent event, media coverage, etc.), and \(r\) the objective attributes for earthquakes to be more likely to happen, such as being located right on seismic faults. Note that \(\pi\), the objective probability of an earthquake, is included in \(i\) in our case, since maps are provided and publicly available. Then the Hedonic Price Function is given by: [ P = P(Z, r, p(i, r)), ] with \(P\) denoting the price of the house and Z denoting an additional set of structural, environmental and locational characteristics not related to the seismic hazards maps. Following Brookshire et al. (1985), the decision of agents is modeled using a state dependent expected utlility function: [ EU = p(i, r) U^E[Z, r, Q] + (1 - p(i, r)) U^{NE}[Z, r, Q], ] where \(U^E(.)\) is the homeowner’s utility in a state in which a earthquake occurs and \(U^NE(.)\) is the homeowner’s utility when there is no such event. \(Q\) is a composite commodity, and the budget constraint of the homeowner is given by: [ M = P(Z, r, p(i, r)) + Q ] Maximising expected utility with respect to \(p\) subject to the budget constraint and then dividing through by the expected marginal utility of income results in the following expression: \[ \frac{\partial P}{\partial p} = \frac{U^{E} - U^{NE}} { p(i,r)\frac{\partial U^{E}}{\partial Q} + \left(1 - p(i,r)\right)\frac{\partial U^{NE}}{\partial Q} } \]

% Data

I use data from , one the biggest real estate companies in Japan. The data consists in listings, covering all prefectures in Japan, from ?? 2015 to ?? 2023. The dataset contain ??? billions of observations, each includeind the house properties such as price and exact geolocation, among many other item (see Apendix for data details). While prices are only about estimation, the data also contains time the ad is available, which I will use as another outcome.

The risk maps are available from the . A webite was created in 2009 to inform people about seism risk, and everyone can take a look at the maps, easily downloadable. They have been updloaded every year since 2009 (expect with 2015). The maps have a high image resolution of 250 meters. There are many seismic risk measures. I choose to use the “probability that each site will be affected by an earthquake of seismic intensity 6 lower or more within 30 years,” as this one is described as representative by the J-SHIS itself (J-SHIS, insérer la réf ici).

To complete the above data, I use some other sources of data. First, I use data from which provides information about demographics, education, health and the labour market in Japan, aggregated at the city level. Data from the population census are available for 2015 and 2020. Other data are missing for some years. To deal with this issue, I use three ways. I use linear interpolation for the census missing values, linear regression and the nearest values for the other missing values. For example, number of habitants for 2023 is derived from the values of 2015 and 2020, number of migrants is derived using linear regression and the number of hospitals for a missing year takes the value of the previous year not missing. See Apendix for each case.

Macroeconomic variables are to take into account when it comes to the housing market. One of the most important is interest rate. Japan’s interest rate was extremely stable throughout the period covered by our data, around 1%. Additional macroeconomic variables include growth domestic product and “Indexes of Business Conditions” provided by e-stat. I choose the lagging index, see Apendix for data details.

% Sample selection I make a number of sample restrictions. I only keep observations that are about houses or appartments, discarding for example shop rents or parking sales. I decide to focus only on the private market, discarding public housing, and I also drop observations that consist in temporary accomodation such hotels or dormitories. You may find details about sample selection in the Apendix.

Risk maps are available each year. I first merge the Lifful Homes dataset with all the maps for every year. Then, I associate each observation to the right map according to the listing publication year.
